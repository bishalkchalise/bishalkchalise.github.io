Working Projects

Local Politics and (mis)allocation of public resources
Abstract
Partisan bias favoring local politicians aligned with the ruling party can lead to public resource misallocation, yet the distributional consequences of such bias remain unclear. We examine this question using Nepal's post-earthquake housing reconstruction program. Employing a close-election regression discontinuity design with comprehensive administrative data of nearly one million household records and 2.3 million grant payment records, we demonstrate that ruling-party-aligned local politicians deliver substantially more aid than opposition-aligned counterparts. Ruling politicians enroll 40 percent more new beneficiaries, with effects concentrated in areas with greatest reconstruction demand: poor rural regions and earthquake-damaged locations. This redistribution operates through the program's grievance redressal system, which grants local politicians discretionary power to respond to affected households' needs. While incumbent candidates and their parties receive higher vote shares in subsequent elections as a reward, this electoral benefit is insufficient to affect re-election likelihood.
Devolution, Diversity and Disorder
Abstract
Political boundaries, as human artifacts, could shape deep-rooted social behaviors and identities. I utilise the merger of municipality boundaries that exogenously altered the ethnic divisions to examine the effect on social tension and conflict events. I find that areas with change in ethnic diversity, measured with polarisation, saw increased instances of violence post-merger by as much as 100 percent and take a U-shape, meaning violence subdues after few years. However, areas with strong social networks witnessed reduced conflicts by as much as 5–20 percent. The paper provides suggestive evidence that alternative social platforms that transcend ethnic groups and provide common economic benefits might be instrumental in moderating ethnic tension and fostering peace.
